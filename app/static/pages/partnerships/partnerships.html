Empowering students in every community

Overcrowded classrooms, scarce materials, outdated teaching methods and excessive punishments prevent students in underserved communities from acquiring the knowledge they need to be successful later in life.

It’s a problem that isn’t limited to developing countries. In the United States, low-income and rural students consistently show major achievement gaps compared to their more affluent peers.

The Oppia Foundation believes these gaps can be narrowed by ensuring that all students have access to a set of free, engaging, and demonstrably effective lessons that they can use at any time.

Our Approach

We want to give students of all backgrounds a chance to learn the skills they need to build brighter futures for themselves and their communities.

In collaboration with our global community of educators, we are building a library of free lessons that provide personalized feedback, thus providing any student around the world with an experience that mimics one-on-one tutoring. We plan to use techniques like machine learning to make Oppia’s guidance for students as specific and useful as possible.

By raising awareness of these lessons, so that students who need them know that they can use them to learn effectively, we hope to remove as many barriers as possible between students and the education they deserve.

Evidence of our success

In 2018, we conducted a randomized trial of our Fractions lessons with students from Delhi, India. Across topics encountered by all students in the study, we found that students in the Oppia treatment group scored significantly better on the post-test than their control-group counterparts. 100% of the students said that they would recommend Oppia lessons to their friends.

Furthermore, user studies with students around the world (including places as diverse as the USA, India, Cameroon, Portugal and Zimbabwe) repeatedly show that they enjoy the Oppia lessons and find them useful.

“I liked learning [with Oppia] on mobile because we get very good questions and, if we get something wrong, we are told how to correct it.” - Student from India, age 9
